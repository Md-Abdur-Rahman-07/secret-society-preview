Skull and Bones

Exploring the Members, Rituals, and Influence of a Secret Society

Origins and History

Skull and Bones was founded in 1832 at Yale University by William Huntington Russell and Alphonso Taft. The society, also known as the Order or Chapter 322, was created to provide an elite group of students with exclusive opportunities for networking and influence.

The society's lore includes rumors of connections to older European secret societies and claims that its roots tie back to German university organizations.

The society is based in "The Tomb," its infamous windowless building on the Yale campus, which has been shrouded in secrecy since its inception.

Membership

Selection Process

Every spring, 15 new members are "tapped" during Yale University's junior year. Selection is based on leadership qualities, academic excellence, and familial connections.

Notable Members
  • George H.W. Bush: The 41st President of the United States and a member of the class of 1948.
  • George W. Bush: The 43rd President of the United States, tapped in 1968.
  • William Howard Taft: The 27th President and later Chief Justice of the United States, a member of the 1878 cohort.
Demographics

Originally male-only, Skull and Bones began admitting women in the 1990s. Its membership has expanded to include diverse backgrounds, reflecting societal changes.

Rituals and Traditions

Initiation Ceremonies

While the exact details of initiation remain secret, rumors suggest bizarre and theatrical rituals, including mock sacrifices and confessions of personal secrets.

Symbols and Artifacts

The skull and bones symbol, often accompanied by the number 322, is the society's emblem. The meaning of "322" is debated but is often linked to Ancient Greek traditions or the death of Demosthenes in 322 BCE.

Influence in Politics and Society

Political Circles

Members of Skull and Bones have held key roles in U.S. government, shaping policies in the White House, Congress, and the Supreme Court. The society has been accused of fostering a "good old boys" network that prioritizes its members' success.

Networking and Power

The society is often seen as a stepping stone to leadership in business and politics, with connections extending into influential organizations like the CIA and major financial institutions.

Legacy and Modern-Day Relevance

Despite its secrecy, Skull and Bones continues to captivate the public imagination. Its members' influence in shaping modern America is undeniable, but its role has diminished in an era of greater transparency and democratization.

Still, the society remains a symbol of elitism and power, and its legacy is cemented in U.S. history.
